## History_1: History

**Q:** Trace the evolution of Indian nationalism from 1857 to 1947.

**Interpretation:** Historical Analysis (25%) | Chronological Evolution | Examiner: Constitutional Scholar

**Quality:** 6/10 (good) | **Words:** 1453

**Introduction with Context and Constitutional Framework**

The evolution of Indian nationalism between 1857 and 1947 represents one of the most complex and multifaceted anti-colonial movements in modern history. Far from being a linear progression, Indian nationalism evolved through distinct phases — from the spontaneous, feudal-led uprising of 1857 to the mass-based, ideologically diverse movement that culminated in independence in 1947. Understanding this trajectory is essential not merely as a historical exercise but because the constitutional architecture of independent India — particularly Articles 73, 74, and 75 governing executive power, Articles 309-311 regulating civil services, and Articles 148-149 establishing the Comptroller and Auditor General — was fundamentally shaped by the nationalist experience of colonial misgovernance. The rule of law, transparency, and accountability that the Constitution enshrines were direct responses to the arbitrary, extractive, and unaccountable nature of British colonial administration. The Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) judgment, which expanded Article 21 to include due process, and Vineet Narain v. Union of India (1998), which laid down guidelines for CBI autonomy, both reflect the enduring constitutional anxiety about executive overreach — an anxiety rooted in the colonial experience that the nationalist movement sought to overcome.

**Phase One: The Revolt of 1857 and Its Aftermath — The Seedbed of National Consciousness**

The Revolt of 1857, often mischaracterised as merely a "sepoy mutiny," was in reality the first large-scale, multi-class challenge to British authority in India. It brought together dispossessed rulers like Nana Sahib and Begum Hazrat Mahal, feudal landlords, peasants, artisans, and religious leaders under a shared grievance against colonial exploitation. However, the revolt lacked a coherent nationalist ideology, a unified command structure, and a vision for an alternative political order. Its failure led to the dissolution of the East India Company and the imposition of direct Crown rule through the Government of India Act, 1858. The aftermath was critical: the British adopted a policy of divide and rule, co-opted the princely states, and systematically excluded Indians from higher administrative positions. This exclusion, particularly from the Indian Civil Service, became a rallying point for the next generation of nationalists. The revolt's legacy was paradoxical — it demonstrated both the possibility of collective resistance and the dangers of disunity, lessons that profoundly shaped subsequent nationalist strategy.

**Phase Two: The Moderate Phase and Institutional Nationalism (1885–1905)**

The founding of the Indian National Congress in 1885 marked the institutionalisation of Indian nationalism. Leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji, Gopal Krishna Gokhale, and Surendranath Banerjee operated within a constitutionalist framework, petitioning the British for incremental reforms — greater Indian representation in legislative councils, civil service reforms, and fiscal accountability. Naoroji's "drain theory" provided an intellectual foundation for economic nationalism, arguing that British rule was systematically transferring Indian wealth to Britain. This phase was characterised by faith in British justice and the liberal tradition, but it also exposed the limits of constitutional agitation. The partition of Bengal in 1905 by Lord Curzon, ostensibly for administrative efficiency but clearly designed to divide Hindus and Bengalis, shattered moderate faith in British benevolence and catalysed the next, more radical phase.

**Phase Three: The Extremist and Swadeshi Movement (1905–1919)**

The Swadeshi movement transformed nationalism from an elite, petitionary exercise into a mass phenomenon. Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Bipin Chandra Pal, and Lala Lajpat Rai championed swaraj, swadeshi, national education, and boycott as tools of self-reliance. Tilak's declaration that "swaraj is my birthright" marked a decisive shift from seeking reforms within the colonial framework to demanding self-rule. The movement saw the emergence of indigenous industries, national schools, and a cultural renaissance that linked political freedom with civilisational pride. However, the movement also revealed internal tensions — between Hindus and Muslims, between moderates and extremists, and between the educated elite and the masses. The British exploited these divisions through the Morley-Minto Reforms of 1909, which introduced separate electorates for Muslims, institutionalising communal representation in a way that would have devastating long-term consequences.

**Phase Four: Gandhi and the Era of Mass Nationalism (1919–1942)**

Mahatma Gandhi's arrival fundamentally redefined Indian nationalism. His philosophy of non-violent non-cooperation, satyagraha, and constructive work transformed the movement from an urban, elite affair into a genuinely mass-based struggle involving peasants, workers, women, and lower castes. The Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22), the Civil Disobedience Movement (1930–34), and the Quit India Movement (1942) each represented escalating challenges to British authority. Gandhi's genius lay in linking political freedom with social reform — the abolition of untouchability, promotion of khadi, Hindu-Muslim unity, and village self-sufficiency. Yet this period also witnessed the rise of competing nationalisms. Muhammad Ali Jinnah's Two-Nation Theory, Subhas Chandra Bose's militant nationalism through the Indian National Army, and B.R. Ambedkar's Dalit movement each challenged the Congress's claim to represent all Indians. The Government of India Act, 1935, while granting provincial autonomy, retained British control over critical areas, and the failure of the Cripps Mission in 1942 demonstrated the irreconcilability of British imperial interests with Indian aspirations.

**Phase Five: Partition, Independence, and the Bitter Triumph (1942–1947)**

The final phase was marked by the collapse of British legitimacy during and after World War II, the Royal Indian Navy Mutiny of 1946, the Cabinet Mission's failed attempt to preserve Indian unity, and the eventual acceptance of partition by the Congress leadership. The Indian Independence Act of 1947 created two dominions but at the cost of unprecedented communal violence, displacement, and the unresolved question of Kashmir. The nationalist movement achieved its primary objective — the end of colonial rule — but at a price that continues to shape South Asian geopolitics.

**Critical Evaluation — Balanced View with Counter-Arguments**

A critical evaluation must acknowledge that Indian nationalism was never monolithic. The dominant Congress narrative has often marginalised the contributions of revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh, socialist and communist movements, tribal uprisings, and the role of women. Furthermore, the movement's failure to prevent partition raises uncomfortable questions about whether the Congress leadership adequately addressed Muslim anxieties or whether Jinnah's demand for Pakistan was an inevitable outcome of colonial divide-and-rule policies. Some historians, particularly from the Cambridge School, argue that Indian nationalism was largely an elite phenomenon driven by competition for jobs and power rather than genuine mass mobilisation. While this critique has merit in highlighting the self-interest of certain leaders, it underestimates the genuine ideological commitment and sacrifice that characterised the movement at every level. The constitutional principles embedded in independent India's governance framework — the emphasis on accountability through institutions like the CAG under Articles 148-149, the protection of civil servants under Articles 309-311, and the separation of powers under the basic structure doctrine — reflect a conscious effort to build a governance system that would prevent the kind of arbitrary, unaccountable rule that characterised the colonial state.

**Way Forward — Specific, Numbered Recommendations**

1. **Integrate subaltern perspectives into mainstream historical pedagogy** — The contributions of Dalits, Adivasis, women, and revolutionaries must be given equal weight in educational curricula to present a more complete picture of the nationalist movement.

2. **Strengthen institutional accountability mechanisms** — Drawing from the nationalist experience of colonial arbitrariness, contemporary governance must reinforce the independence of constitutional bodies like the CAG, Election Commission, and CBI, as reinforced by the Vineet Narain guidelines.

3. **Address the legacy of partition through people-to-people diplomacy** — India-Pakistan relations remain hostage to 1947; Track II diplomacy and cultural exchanges can help heal historical wounds.

4. **Promote e-governance as a tool of democratic accountability** — Schemes like PRAGATI, MyGov, and CPGRAMS represent the modern equivalent of the nationalist demand for transparent, responsive governance and must be strengthened and expanded.

5. **Reform civil services to reflect constitutional values** — The lateral entry debate must be resolved in a way that balances merit with social justice, ensuring that the bureaucracy serves the constitutional mandate of equality and accountability rather than becoming an unaccountable elite.

**Conclusion — Forward-Looking Synthesis**

The evolution of Indian nationalism from 1857 to 1947 was not merely a political movement but a profound civilisational reckoning — a process through which a diverse, fragmented society forged a collective identity in opposition to colonial exploitation. The constitutional framework that emerged from this struggle, with its emphasis on rule of law, separation of powers, and institutional accountability, was designed to ensure that the governance failures of the colonial era would never be repeated. Yet, as contemporary challenges — from the transparency concerns raised by the Electoral Bonds judgment to the ongoing debate over lateral entry in civil services — demonstrate, the nationalist project of building a just, accountable, and inclusive governance system remains incomplete. History teaches us that nationalism, divorced from social justice and institutional integrity, risks becoming exclusionary. The true legacy of 1857–1947 lies not in uncritical celebration but in the ongoing struggle to realise the constitutional promises that the nationalist movement bequeathed to independent India.

## History_2: History

**Q:** Discuss the impact of British colonial rule on Indian agriculture.

**Interpretation:** Historical Analysis (58%) | Chronological Evolution | Examiner: Constitutional Scholar

**Quality:** 5/10 (adequate) | **Words:** 1519

[Introduction with context and constitutional framework]

The transformation of Indian agriculture under British colonial rule represents one of the most consequential structural shifts in the subcontinent's economic history, the reverberations of which continue to shape contemporary agrarian policy debates, land reform legislation, and rural governance frameworks. Understanding this colonial legacy is not merely an exercise in historical analysis but carries direct constitutional relevance, as the framers of the Indian Constitution embedded principles of agrarian justice, equitable resource distribution, and state responsibility toward rural welfare precisely as a corrective response to colonial exploitation. Articles 38 and 39 of the Directive Principles of State Policy, which mandate the state to promote the welfare of the people and ensure that the operation of the economic system does not result in the concentration of wealth, were conceived as antidotes to the extractive colonial agrarian model. The colonial period fundamentally restructured Indian agriculture from a subsistence-oriented, community-managed system into a commodity-driven, revenue-extractive apparatus designed to serve imperial economic interests, and this metamorphosis demands careful examination across multiple dimensions.

[Body with multi-dimensional analysis]

The most immediate and devastating impact of British colonial rule on Indian agriculture was the imposition of exploitative land revenue systems that systematically drained rural wealth. The Permanent Settlement of 1793, introduced by Lord Cornwallis in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa, created a class of zamindars as intermediaries who were tasked with collecting fixed revenue from peasants, regardless of actual agricultural output or climatic conditions. This system, while providing revenue certainty to the colonial state, created a parasitic landlord class that extracted surplus from cultivators without any obligation to invest in land improvement. The Ryotwari system in Madras and Bombay Presidencies and the Mahalwari system in the North-Western Provinces, though differing in their mechanisms, shared the common feature of extracting revenue directly or indirectly from actual cultivators at rates that were often confiscatory. The revenue demands were typically set at fifty to sixty percent of gross produce, leaving peasants with barely enough for subsistence. This relentless extraction impoverished the agrarian economy, destroyed traditional systems of communal land management, and created a cycle of indebtedness that forced peasants into the clutches of moneylenders. The Deccan Riots of 1875 were a direct consequence of this agrarian distress, as indebted ryots rose against Marwari moneylenders who had become the new instruments of colonial extraction. The constitutional response to this legacy is visible in the extensive land reform legislations undertaken by states in the post-independence period, the abolition of zamindari systems, and the eventual inclusion of land reform provisions in the Ninth Schedule to protect them from judicial review.

The second transformative impact was the forced commercialisation of Indian agriculture, which reoriented farming from food security to cash crop production for imperial markets. The British systematically encouraged and coerced the cultivation of indigo, cotton, jute, opium, tea, and other export-oriented crops at the expense of food grains. The infamous indigo revolt of 1859-1860 in Bengal exposed the brutal coercion employed by planters who forced ryots to cultivate indigo under the tinkathia system, receiving advances that trapped them in perpetual debt. The opium trade with China, which financed a significant portion of colonial revenue, was sustained through compulsory cultivation in Bihar and Malwa. This commercialisation disrupted traditional crop patterns, reduced the area under food cultivation, and made Indian agriculture dependent on volatile international commodity markets. The consequences were catastrophic: the Great Bengal Famine of 1770, which killed an estimated ten million people, the Madras Famine of 1876-1878, and the Bengal Famine of 1943, which claimed approximately three million lives, were all exacerbated by colonial policies that prioritised revenue extraction and export over food security. The Bengal Famine of 1943, in particular, occurred not due to absolute food shortage but because of wartime procurement policies, speculative hoarding, and the deliberate denial policy implemented by the colonial administration. This historical trauma directly informed the constitutional emphasis on food security, eventually culminating in the National Food Security Act of 2013 and the Right to Food campaign, which trace their moral and political origins to the colonial failure to protect the most basic right of the Indian peasant.

The third dimension of colonial impact was the systematic destruction of traditional irrigation systems, rural industries, and the self-sufficient village economy that had characterised pre-colonial India. The British dismantled the sophisticated network of tanks, wells, and community-managed irrigation systems that had sustained Indian agriculture for centuries, replacing them with a centralised, revenue-focused approach that neglected maintenance and expansion. The decline of rural handicrafts and cottage industries, destroyed by the influx of British manufactured goods, eliminated the supplementary income sources that had sustained agrarian communities during lean agricultural seasons. This de-industrialisation of the countryside forced an ever-increasing population to depend solely on agriculture, leading to fragmentation of holdings, overcrowding on land, and declining per capita agricultural output. The famous observation by Karl Marx that the British had "destroyed the spinning wheel and the loom" in India captures this dual destruction of both agricultural and industrial self-sufficiency. The colonial state's investment in irrigation, such as the canal systems in Punjab and the Godavari and Krishna delta systems, was primarily designed to expand the area under revenue-yielding cash crops rather than to enhance food security or peasant welfare. The contemporary relevance of this legacy is evident in the persistent challenges of rural distress, farmer suicides, and the ongoing debate around minimum support prices, both of which are rooted in the structural vulnerabilities created by colonial agrarian policies.

[Critical Evaluation]

While the overwhelmingly extractive nature of colonial agrarian policy is well-documented, a balanced assessment must acknowledge certain counter-arguments and complexities. Some economic historians, notably Morris David Morris, have argued that the colonial period also introduced certain institutional innovations, including the codification of property rights, the establishment of agricultural research stations, and the development of railway infrastructure that, despite being designed for resource extraction, eventually facilitated market integration for Indian farmers. The Punjab canal colonies, for instance, transformed barren land into productive agricultural zones, though the benefits accrued disproportionately to loyalist landlords rather than ordinary cultivators. Furthermore, it would be analytically incomplete to attribute all of India's contemporary agrarian problems solely to colonialism. Post-independence policy failures, including the slow pace of land reform implementation, inadequate investment in agricultural research and extension services, the distortions introduced by the Green Revolution's focus on wheat and rice at the expense of millets and pulses, and the persistent neglect of rainfed agriculture, have compounded colonial-era vulnerabilities. The current crisis of groundwater depletion, soil degradation, and climate vulnerability in Indian agriculture is as much a product of post-independence policy choices as of colonial legacies. Nevertheless, the foundational structural distortions, the creation of a landlord class divorced from productive investment, the orientation of agriculture toward external markets, and the systematic underinvestment in rural welfare were unmistakably colonial contributions that the Indian state has been attempting to rectify for over seven decades.

[Way Forward]

Addressing the enduring colonial legacy in Indian agriculture requires a multi-pronged strategy that combines historical awareness with contemporary policy innovation. First, land records must be comprehensively digitised and updated, building on the Digital India Land Records Modernisation Programme, to eliminate the ambiguities in land tenure that originated in colonial-era settlement operations and continue to disadvantage marginal farmers, particularly women and tribal communities. Second, agricultural diversification must be actively promoted through procurement support for millets, pulses, and oilseeds, reducing the colonial-era dependence on monoculture and cash crops that persists in regions like Punjab and Haryana. Third, investment in community-managed irrigation systems must be prioritised over large dam projects, reviving the pre-colonial tradition of local water management through institutions like the traditional tank systems of Tamil Nadu and the phad system of Maharashtra. Fourth, the e-NAM and other digital market platforms must be strengthened to ensure that farmers receive fair prices, countering the colonial pattern of exploitative intermediation that continues through the mandi system. Fifth, agricultural research must be redirected toward climate-resilient, sustainable farming practices, moving beyond the Green Revolution paradigm that, while successful in addressing immediate food security, created ecological imbalances that echo the colonial neglect of long-term sustainability.

[Conclusion]

The impact of British colonial rule on Indian agriculture was not merely economic exploitation but a fundamental restructuring of the relationship between the state, the cultivator, and the land. The colonial state transformed agriculture from a livelihood system embedded in community networks into a revenue-generating machine serving imperial interests, creating structural distortions that independent India has been grappling with for over seven decades. The constitutional commitment to agrarian justice, the Directive Principles mandating equitable distribution, and the contemporary policy frameworks around food security, land reform, and rural development are all, in essence, responses to this colonial inheritance. Understanding this historical continuum is essential for crafting effective agrarian policy today, for the challenges of farmer distress, land fragmentation, water scarcity, and market exploitation are not merely contemporary policy failures but the deepening of colonial-era fault lines. The path forward lies not in romanticising pre-colonial agriculture nor in perpetuating colonial structures under new names, but in building an agrarian system that is equitable, sustainable, and truly serves the interests of those who feed the nation.

## Geography_1: Geography

**Q:** Discuss the impact of climate change on Indian monsoon patterns.

**Interpretation:** Environmental Governance (24%) | Multi-Dimensional Analysis | Examiner: Environmental Policy Scholar

**Quality:** 7/10 (good) | **Words:** 1531

**Introduction with Context and Constitutional Framework**

Climate change is fundamentally altering the hydrological cycle of the Indian subcontinent, with the monsoon — a phenomenon that defines India's civilizational, economic, and ecological identity — being among the most profoundly affected. The Indian monsoon, governed by land-sea thermal contrast, the Tibetan Plateau's heating, the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), and the Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD), is now exhibiting heightened variability that directly threatens the nation's food security, water availability, and disaster resilience. The constitutional framework for addressing this challenge spans Articles 48A (protection of environment) and 51A(g) (duty to protect natural environment), alongside the Directive Principles and the institutional mechanisms established under the Environment Protection Act, 1986, and the National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC), 2008. The Supreme Court, in landmark interpretations extending Article 21 through Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) — establishing the due process doctrine — has progressively read the right to a clean environment as part of the right to life, thereby obligating the state to proactively address climate-induced monsoon disruptions.

**Body: Multi-Dimensional Analysis**

From a climatological perspective, the Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) data spanning 1951–2023 reveals a discernible shift in monsoon behaviour. While the all-India seasonal rainfall has shown a slight declining trend of approximately 6% over the last six decades, the character of the monsoon has changed more dramatically than the aggregate volume suggests. The phenomenon of "monsoon breaks" has lengthened, and extreme rainfall events — defined as more than 150mm in 24 hours at a station — have increased by nearly 12% per decade since 1950. This intensification is consistent with the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, which predicts approximately 7% increase in atmospheric moisture-holding capacity per degree Celsius of warming. The Indian Ocean is warming faster than the global average, amplifying moisture flux into the monsoon system but simultaneously destabilizing the low-level westerly jet and the monsoon trough, leading to a paradox of more intense but less predictable rainfall. Research published by the Indian Institute of Tropical Meteorology (IITM) and the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Sixth Assessment Report (AR6, 2021) confirms that the frequency of localized, high-intensity precipitation events has increased disproportionately compared to moderate rainfall days, a pattern that directly undermines agricultural planning dependent on evenly distributed monsoon precipitation.

The agricultural dimension is the most consequential, engaging Articles 48 (organization of agriculture) and 21 (right to livelihood through right to life) read with the Directive Principles. Approximately 58% of India's net sown area is rainfed, and nearly 40% of the country's food production depends directly on the southwest monsoon. The shifting monsoon has manifested in delayed onset — the monsoon's arrival over Kerala has shown increased variability, with onset dates deviating by up to two weeks in recent years — and erratic spatial distribution, creating simultaneous droughts in Maharashtra and floods in Assam in the same season. The 2023 Kharif season demonstrated this acutely: deficient rainfall in August across the Indo-Gangetic plains damaged standing paddy crops, while excess rainfall in September caused post-harvest losses. The National Commission on Farmers (Swaminathan Commission, 2006) had already warned that climate variability would be the single greatest threat to Indian agriculture; the current monsoon disruptions validate this prognosis. The Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY), launched in 2016, was designed precisely to address such climate-induced crop losses, yet its implementation — particularly claim settlement delays and actuarial challenges — reveals the gap between policy intent and ground reality.

The urban and infrastructural dimension brings into focus the governance framework under the 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments, which devolve urban planning and disaster management to local bodies. Cities like Mumbai (2005, 2017, 2020), Chennai (2015), and Bengaluru (2022) have experienced catastrophic flooding directly attributable to intensified monsoon rainfall overwhelming colonial-era drainage systems designed for precipitation intensities 50–70% lower than current extremes. The Smart Cities Mission and AMRUT (Atal Mission for Rejuvenation and Urban Transformation) have allocated funds for urban flood resilience, but the pace of infrastructure adaptation lags behind the rate of monsoon intensification. The National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA), operating under the Disaster Management Act, 2005, has issued guidelines for urban flooding, yet compliance remains fragmented across municipalities with limited technical capacity.

The hydrological and water security dimension connects to Article 21's expanded interpretation and the interstate water disputes under Article 263. Climate change is accelerating glacial melt in the Himalayas, increasing the short-term flow of glacier-fed rivers like the Ganga and Brahmaputra while threatening long-term baseflow. The monsoon's increasing concentration into fewer, more intense events reduces groundwater recharge — since soil infiltration capacity is exceeded during extreme downpours, leading to surface runoff rather than aquifer replenishment. The Central Ground Water Board's 2022 assessment shows that 1,186 assessment units are "over-exploited," a crisis compounded by erratic recharge. The Jal Jeevan Mission, launched in 2019 with the objective of providing functional household tap connections to every rural household by 2024, must reckon with this hydrological reality: piped water supply is meaningless without sustainable groundwater sources, which depend fundamentally on predictable monsoon recharge patterns.

From an ecological and biodiversity standpoint, the monsoon's shifting phenology disrupts species migration, breeding cycles, and forest fire regimes. The Wildlife Protection Act, 1972, and the Biological Diversity Act, 2002, provide institutional frameworks, but their implementation often conflates static conservation with the dynamic reality of climate-driven ecological change. The Western Ghats — a biodiversity hotspot and critical monsoon-generating orographic barrier — faces compounded threats from both deforestation and altered precipitation patterns, with cascading consequences for peninsular India's water security.

**Critical Evaluation: Balanced View with Counter-Arguments**

It is essential, however, to avoid environmental determinism in this analysis. While climate change is a significant driver, monsoon variability is also attributable to localized factors: land-use change, aerosol loading from crop stubble burning and industrial emissions, urban heat island effects, and large-scale deforestation. Some climatologists argue that the Indian monsoon's total seasonal rainfall has not declined as dramatically as popularly believed, and that the perceived crisis is partly a function of increased population density and agricultural intensification placing greater demand on the same water resources. The counter-argument that India's monsoon system has always been variable — with historical famines and floods predating industrial-era climate change — holds partial validity but is insufficient: the rate of change in the current epoch is unprecedented in the Holocene record, and the adaptive capacity of 1.4 billion people with extensive rainfed agriculture creates a vulnerability multiplier that historical variability never produced.

Furthermore, India's policy response, while institutionally robust on paper — with eight missions under NAPCC, the State Action Plans on Climate Change (SAPCCs), India's updated Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) submitted in 2022 — suffers from implementation deficits. The gap between the National Adaptation Fund for Climate Change (NAFCC) allocations and actual disbursement, the slow pace of climate-resilient crop variety development, and the persistent subsidization of water-intensive crops like sugarcane and paddy in water-scarce regions all indicate that policy coherence remains elusive.

**Way Forward: Specific, Recommendations**

First, India must invest in hyperlocal monsoon forecasting by expanding the IMD's Doppler Weather Radar network from the current approximately 33 stations to at least 100, integrating artificial intelligence and ensemble modelling for district-level, 15-day probabilistic forecasts accessible to farmers through the mKisan portal and Common Service Centres. Second, watershed management must be reimagined as climate infrastructure: the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) should explicitly prioritize monsoon-resilient water harvesting structures — check dams, percolation tanks, and contour bunding — with climate vulnerability indices determining allocation rather than purely employment-generation metrics. Third, crop diversification incentives under the National Food Security Mission must be linked to agro-climatic zone-specific climate projections, with procurement and minimum support price (MSP) structures revised to incentivize millets, pulses, and oilseeds over water-intensive crops in vulnerable regions. Fourth, urban flood resilience must be mandated through model building bylaws that enforce permeable surface ratios, sponge-city principles, and real-time flood monitoring — the Chennai flood of 2015 demonstrated that even 48 hours of advance warning could have prevented catastrophic losses. Fifth, interstate river basin governance must be strengthened under Article 263 through empowered River Basin Authorities with climate adaptation mandates, moving beyond the current tribunal-centric dispute resolution model to proactive, science-based water allocation that accounts for projected monsoon shifts.

**Conclusion: Forward-Looking Synthesis**

The impact of climate change on the Indian monsoon is not a distant, abstract projection but a present reality that intersects with every dimension of India's development trajectory — from the constitutional guarantee of livelihood and a clean environment to the practical imperatives of food security, urban resilience, and ecological sustainability. India's response must transcend the false dichotomy between development and climate adaptation; the monsoon's increasing variability demands that climate resilience be embedded as a non-negotiable criterion in every infrastructure, agricultural, and urban policy decision. The constitutional architecture — from the expanded interpretation of Article 21 to the decentralized governance mandates of the 73rd and 74th Amendments — provides the legal foundation; what is required is the political will and institutional innovation to translate this framework into adaptive governance at the pace and scale that the changing monsoon demands. The monsoon has always been India's greatest natural endowment; in the era of climate change, managing it wisely may become India's greatest governance challenge.

## Society_1: Society

**Q:** Discuss the challenges of aging population in India.

**Interpretation:** Social Justice & Welfare (21%) | Problem-Solution-Evaluation | Examiner: Social Welfare Analyst

**Quality:** 5/10 (adequate) | **Words:** 972

India is undergoing a profound demographic transition. While often celebrated for its "demographic dividend" of a young population, the nation simultaneously faces the quiet, accelerating crisis of population aging. The challenges of this aging populace are not merely a future concern but a present-day socio-economic reality demanding urgent, nuanced policy intervention.

The constitutional and legal framework provides a foundation for addressing this challenge, though it primarily frames it as an enabling provision, not an enforceable right. Article 41 of the Directive Principles of State Policy enjoins the State to make effective provision for securing the right to work, education, and public assistance in cases of old age, within the limits of its economic capacity. Article 47 speaks to raising the level of nutrition and standard of living. The landmark case of *Indra Sawhney v. Union of India* (1992), while primarily on reservation, established the principle that the State's policy must be guided by the imperative of social justice. This principle is directly applicable to the elderly, a vulnerable group requiring affirmative action. However, the challenge lies in translating these broad directives into concrete, enforceable entitlements.

The multi-dimensional challenges of India's aging population are a complex interplay of socio-economic deprivation, healthcare inadequacy, and social isolation. The most immediate is the crisis of economic insecurity. A vast majority of India's elderly, particularly in rural areas, are not covered by any formal pension scheme. The National Social Assistance Programme (NSAP) provides a meagre pension, often delayed and insufficient to combat inflation. This forces many into a life of dependency or continued, often exploitative, labour, violating the spirit of Article 21's right to live with dignity. The lack of financial independence is a fundamental barrier to accessing other necessities.

This economic fragility is exacerbated by a healthcare system ill-equipped for geriatric care. India's public health infrastructure is primarily designed for maternal and child health and infectious diseases. There is a severe shortage of trained geriatricians, age-friendly hospitals, and affordable long-term care facilities. The Ayushman Bharat scheme, while revolutionary, is primarily focused on secondary and tertiary hospitalization, not the chronic, multi-morbidity management required by the elderly. The out-of-pocket expenditure on health remains catastrophically high for this demographic, pushing families into debt. The recent *Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta* (2018) case, which clarified the creamy layer concept for SC/ST promotions, underscores the judiciary's focus on substantive equality. Applying this lens, the state must move beyond formal equality and provide substantive, targeted healthcare support to its elderly citizens.

Beyond material needs, the challenge of social isolation and mental health is profound. The rapid migration of the youth to urban centres has led to the phenomenon of "empty nests" and "skip-generation" households, particularly in states like Kerala and Tamil Nadu. The elderly, especially women, face neglect, loneliness, and in extreme cases, abuse within the very families meant to be their support system. The Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act, 2007, provides a legal remedy, but its implementation is weak, and the social stigma attached to filing cases against one's children often prevents its use. This social dimension of aging is a direct challenge to the constitutional value of fraternity enshrined in the Preamble.

The critical evaluation of India's response reveals a significant gap between policy intent and ground reality. The government has initiated several schemes, such as the **PM-JANMAN** scheme for tribal communities (which includes elderly welfare components), **Stand-Up India** for entrepreneurial support, and **PM-VISHWAKARMA** for traditional artisans, which can indirectly benefit the elderly by strengthening household economies. However, these are not specifically designed for the elderly. The **SACRED Portal** (Senior Able Citizens for Re-employment in Dignity) is a welcome step towards productive aging, but its scale is limited. The counter-argument often presented is that with pressing issues like poverty and unemployment, prioritizing the elderly is a luxury. This is a flawed premise. Investing in the health and welfare of the elderly is not a diversion of resources but a strengthening of the social fabric. A healthy, secure elderly population can contribute as caregivers, advisors, and consumers, thereby supporting the economic productivity of the working-age population. The failure to act now will lead to a much larger fiscal and social crisis in the future.

The way forward requires a specific, implementable, and multi-pronged strategy. First, there must be a significant enhancement and universalization of the old-age pension under NSAP, indexed to inflation, and its timely disbursement through Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT). # Challenges of Tribal Development in India: A Multi-Dimensional Analysis

## Introduction

Tribal communities in India, constituting approximately 8.6% of the national population (Census 2011) and spread across 705 distinct groups, represent one of the most complex development paradoxes in the Indian republic. Despite constitutional safeguards under Articles 14, 15(4), 16(4), 21, and the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, alongside dedicated institutional mechanisms like the Ministry of Tribal Affairs and the Tribal Sub-Plan, tribal communities continue to lag behind on virtually every human development indicator. The challenges of tribal development are not merely administrative or fiscal — they are deeply structural, rooted in the tension between a homogenising development paradigm and the fundamentally distinct socio-cultural, economic, and ecological realities of tribal life. Understanding these challenges requires moving beyond a deficit-based narrative to interrogate the very frameworks through which "development" has been conceptualised and delivered to India's Adivasi populations.

## The Land Question: Dispossession as the Foundational Challenge

The most fundamental challenge to tribal development lies in the systematic alienation of tribal communities from their land and forest resources — the very basis of their identity, livelihood, and cultural continuity. The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006 (FRA) was enacted precisely to address this historical injustice, yet its implementation remains deeply inadequate. As of 2023, over 50% of individual forest right claims and a significant proportion of community forest right claims have been rejected, often on procedural grounds that reflect bureaucratic hostility rather than legislative intent. The Chhotanagpur Tenancy Act and Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act, which historically protected tribal land from alienation, have been repeatedly diluted — most controversially in Jharkhand in 2016-17, when amendments to allow transfer of tribal land to the government for "development purposes" triggered massive protests and were eventually withdrawn. The fundamental challenge here is epistemological: the state's conception of land as a fungible economic asset collides with the tribal understanding of land as a sacred, communal, and identity-anchoring resource. Large infrastructure projects — dams, mining operations, and industrial corridors — continue to displace tribal communities with inadequate rehabilitation. The Narmada Bachao Andolan and the ongoing resistance to bauxite mining in Niyamgiri (Odisha) exemplify this structural conflict. The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013, while an improvement, has been weakened by subsequent amendments that exempt several categories of projects from its purview, disproportionately affecting tribal areas rich in mineral resources.

The constitutional architecture for tribal governance — particularly the Fifth Schedule (applicable in 10 states) and Sixth Schedule (in Northeast India) — provides a robust framework, yet its operationalisation reveals a persistent governance deficit. The PESA Act, 1996, which extends Panchayati Raj to Scheduled Areas with special provisions for Gram Sabha empowerment, remains unimplemented in letter and spirit across most states. Gram Sabhas, which are supposed to be the primary decision-making bodies regarding land, resources, and development projects in Scheduled Areas, are routinely bypassed or reduced to rubber-stamp institutions. The Tribes Advisory Councils, mandated under the Fifth Schedule, have been criticised for being advisory bodies without enforcement power, often dominated by non-tribal bureaucrats. The recent PM-JANMAN (Janjati Adivasi Nyaya Maha Abhiyan) scheme, launched in 2023 with an outlay of approximately ₹24,000 crore targeting 11 critical interventions across 75 Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs), represents a significant policy commitment. The human development gap between tribal and non-tribal populations remains stark and, in some dimensions, widening. The Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS), established to provide quality education in tribal areas, have expanded but remain insufficient in number and variable in quality. Perhaps the most insidious challenge to tribal development is the slow erosion of cultural identity, which operates through multiple vectors. The conversion of tribal populations — estimated at significant scale in Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and parts of the Northeast — raises complex questions about religious freedom, cultural preservation, and the role of external influences. The dominance of mainstream media, the market economy, and the formal education system creates what scholars have termed "cultural hegemony," where tribal languages, knowledge systems, artistic traditions, and social structures are progressively devalued. Of the 197 languages classified as "endangered" by UNESCO in India, a disproportionate number are tribal languages. The loss of language is not merely a cultural phenomenon — it represents the erosion of entire epistemological frameworks, ecological knowledge systems, and community governance traditions. The challenge for development policy is to enable material progress without cultural annihilation — a balance that India's development paradigm has historically failed to achieve.

## The Political Economy of Exploitation and Left-Wing Extremism

Tribal areas, particularly in the "Red Corridor" spanning central and eastern India, remain theatres of Left-Wing Extremism (LWE), which is itself both a cause and consequence of developmental failure. The concentration of mineral wealth in tribal areas — approximately 50% of India's coal, 80% of its bauxite, and significant deposits of iron ore and manganese — creates a political economy where tribal communities are simultaneously the most resource-rich and the most impoverished populations. The exploitation of tribal labour, including bonded labour (prohibited under Article 23 but persisting in practice), the operation of exploitative middlemen in Minor Forest Produce (MFP) trade, and the capture of tribal political representation by non-tribal interests all constitute structural barriers to development. It is essential to critically examine whether the dominant welfare-oriented approach to tribal development is itself part of the problem. The framing of tribal communities as "backward" populations requiring "upliftment" carries an implicit civilisational hierarchy that undermines tribal agency and self-determination. The constitutional vision — particularly the spirit of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules and PESA — was not merely welfare-oriented but envisioned tribal communities as autonomous political entities with the right to govern themselves according to their own customs and institutions. The persistent failure to implement PESA in full, the dilution of land protection laws, and the top-down design of development schemes all suggest that the state's approach has been more extractive than emancipatory. At the same time, it would be intellectually dishonest to romanticise tribal isolation or to suggest that tribal communities are uniformly opposed to development. The challenge is to distinguish between development that is imposed and development that is chosen — between integration that is coercive and integration that is consensual. The success of community-led conservation initiatives, such as the Mendha-Lekha village in Maharashtra's Gadchiroli district, which exercised its community forest rights under FRA to sustainably manage and profit from its forests, demonstrates that tribal communities are not passive recipients but active agents of their own development when given genuine autonomy.

## Way Forward

First, the full and faithful implementation of PESA across all Scheduled Areas must be treated as a non-negotiable governance priority, with Gram Sabhas empowered with genuine decision-making authority over land, resources, and development projects, backed by statutory enforcement mechanisms rather than mere advisory status. Second, the FRA implementation must be overhauled with a presumption in favour of claimants, time-bound disposal of rejected claims with mandatory appellate mechanisms, and the active involvement of tribal communities in the verification process rather than reliance solely on bureaucratic assessment. Third, a dedicated Tribal Education Policy must be formulated — distinct from the general National Education Policy framework — that mandates mother-tongue-based multilingual education up to Class VIII in tribal areas, integrates tribal knowledge systems into curricula, and ensures that Eklavya schools are staffed with culturally competent educators. Fourth, the political economy of MFP must be restructured by ensuring that MSP for MFP is set at genuinely remunerative levels, primary collection is conducted by Gram Sabhas or tribal cooperatives rather than middlemen, and value addition happens within tribal areas through tribal-owned enterprises. Fifth, the governance of Scheduled Areas must be reformed to ensure that Tribal Advisory Councils have binding rather than merely advisory powers, and that the Governor's special responsibilities under the Fifth Schedule are exercised with genuine accountability to tribal communities rather than to the state political executive. Sixth, a comprehensive tribal health strategy must integrate traditional healing systems with formal healthcare, deploy mobile health units with tribal-language capacity in remote areas, and address sickle cell anaemia through universal screening and genetic counselling in endemic regions.

## Conclusion

The challenges of tribal development in India are not merely technical or administrative — they are fundamentally questions about the nature of the Indian state's relationship with its most marginalised citizens. The constitutional framework, from the equality provisions of Articles 14 and 15(4) to the protective discrimination mechanisms of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, provides a robust foundation, but its promise remains unfulfilled because the dominant development paradigm continues to treat tribal communities as objects of policy rather than subjects of their own destiny. The path forward requires not just more resources or better schemes — though both are necessary — but a paradigmatic shift from a welfare-based, top-down model to one grounded in tribal self-determination, cultural respect, and genuine political autonomy. Until the Indian state is willing to share power with tribal communities rather than merely delivering services to them, the development gap will persist not despite policy, but because of its fundamental design.

Union of India* (1978) established that any procedure affecting life or liberty must be just, fair, and reasonable—a principle that now extends to algorithmic decision-making. Similarly, *Vineet Narain v. Union of India* (1998) underscored the need for institutional autonomy and transparency, principles that are challenged when opaque AI systems operate without oversight. As India advances initiatives like Digital India, PRAGATI, and MyGov, the ethical governance of AI becomes not merely a technical concern but a constitutional imperative.

One of the most pressing ethical challenges is algorithmic bias, which threatens the principle of equality under Article 14. AI systems trained on historical data often replicate and amplify existing social inequities. For instance, predictive policing tools used in some Indian states have disproportionately targeted marginalized communities due to biased training datasets rooted in past discriminatory enforcement patterns. This creates a feedback loop where AI entrenches systemic injustice under the guise of neutrality. The absence of mandatory algorithmic impact assessments—akin to environmental impact assessments—means such biases go undetected until they cause tangible harm. Furthermore, the lack of diversity in AI development teams exacerbates this issue, as developers may not fully grasp the socio-cultural nuances of India’s pluralistic society.

Transparency and explainability constitute another core ethical dilemma. Many AI models, particularly deep learning systems, function as “black boxes,” making decisions that even their creators cannot fully interpret. When such systems are deployed in welfare distribution—such as identifying beneficiaries under the National Food Security Act—the inability to explain why a household was excluded violates the citizen’s right to reasoned administrative action. The CPGRAMS portal, designed to enhance grievance redressal, becomes ineffective if citizens cannot understand or challenge AI-driven decisions. This opacity also undermines accountability under Articles 75 and 164, which hold the Council of Ministers collectively responsible to the legislature. If an AI system denies a pension or flags a citizen as a fraud risk without clear justification, who bears moral and legal responsibility—the algorithm, the bureaucrat, or the minister?

Privacy and surveillance represent a third dimension of ethical concern. AI-powered facial recognition systems, increasingly adopted by police forces across India, operate in a legal vacuum. Despite the Supreme Court’s recognition of privacy as a fundamental right in *K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (2017), there is no comprehensive data protection law to regulate state use of biometric data. The Personal Data Protection Bill, though introduced, remains unenacted, leaving citizens vulnerable to mass surveillance. The Aadhaar ecosystem, while transformative for service delivery, has also enabled unprecedented data aggregation, raising fears of a surveillance state. The ethical tension lies in balancing national security and administrative efficiency against the citizen’s right to informational self-determination.

Moreover, the deployment of AI in governance risks deepening the digital divide. While e-Governance platforms like MyGov and PRAGATI aim to enhance citizen participation, they presuppose digital literacy and internet access—privileges not uniformly distributed across rural and urban India. Elderly citizens, persons with disabilities, and those in remote areas may be excluded from AI-mediated services, violating the principle of inclusive governance. The recent push for AI in electoral processes—such as voter verification through facial recognition—has sparked concerns about disenfranchisement, especially when errors occur in biometric authentication.

Critically, proponents argue that AI can reduce human discretion, thereby curbing corruption and enhancing objectivity. For example, AI-driven analysis of tax returns has improved detection of evasion, contributing to fiscal integrity. However, this utilitarian argument overlooks the dehumanization of governance. When a farmer’s crop insurance claim is denied by an algorithm without human review, the system fails the test of empathy and contextual understanding. The ethical framework must therefore balance efficiency with equity, automation with accountability.

To address these challenges, a multi-pronged strategy is essential. First, the government should enact a robust AI Governance Act mandating algorithmic transparency, bias audits, and human oversight for all public-sector AI applications. Second, an independent AI Ethics Commission—modeled on the Comptroller and Auditor General’s office under Articles 148–149—should be established to audit AI systems and ensure compliance with constitutional norms. Third, capacity building is crucial: civil servants must be trained in AI ethics through institutions like LBSNAA, integrating modules on digital rights and algorithmic accountability. Fourth, participatory design involving civil society, technologists, and marginalized communities should be institutionalized to co-create inclusive AI solutions. Finally, the judiciary must evolve its interpretive framework to recognize algorithmic harm as a violation of fundamental rights, building on the *Puttaswamy* precedent.

In conclusion, AI in governance is not inherently unethical, but its unregulated deployment risks undermining the very democratic values it seeks to serve. The path forward lies not in resisting technological change, but in embedding constitutional morality into the architecture of AI systems. As India aspires to be a global leader in digital governance, it must ensure that innovation serves justice, not just efficiency. The ethical governance of AI is not a technical afterthought—it is the new frontier of constitutionalism.

## Essay_1: Essay

Technology, as a force multiplier of human capability, has always carried within it the simultaneous potential for emancipation and subjugation. From the printing press that democratized knowledge to the nuclear bomb that threatened civilizational annihilation, every technological leap has demanded a corresponding evolution in governance, ethics, and constitutional morality. In the Indian context, this duality is not merely philosophical but deeply constitutional — implicating Articles 14, 19(1)(a), 21, 38, and 39 in ways that demand rigorous analysis. The question of whether technology is a double-edged sword is, therefore, not a binary inquiry but a governance challenge that tests the state's capacity to harness innovation while safeguarding fundamental rights.

The emancipatory edge of technology is most visible in its capacity to realize socio-economic rights that the Constitution envisions but the state has struggled to deliver. Article 38 obligates the state to promote the welfare of the people, and Article 39 directs policy toward ensuring adequate means of livelihood. Digital India, BharatNet, and DigiLocker have operationalized these directive principles by collapsing geographical and bureaucratic barriers. A farmer in rural Odisha accessing PM-KISAN benefits through Aadhaar-linked Direct Benefit Transfer, a woman in Bastar filing a grievance through the UMANG platform, or a student in Ladakh accessing SWAYAM courses — these are not incremental improvements but structural transformations in the state-citizen relationship. The Supreme Court in Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Aadhaar) v. Union of India (2018) acknowledged this potential, upholding Aadhaar's validity for welfare delivery while simultaneously circumscribing its scope to prevent surveillance overreach. Technology, in this dimension, becomes the instrument through which Article 21's expanded interpretation — encompassing the right to livelihood, dignity, and a meaningful life — is made tangible for millions who were previously excluded from the state's reach.

Yet the coercive edge of technology is equally stark and constitutionally alarming. The same Aadhaar architecture that enables welfare delivery also creates the infrastructure for mass surveillance, a concern that Justice D.Y. Chandrachud's dissenting opinion in the Aadhaar judgment articulated with prescient clarity. The Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) judgment, while establishing the right to privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21, simultaneously exposed the vulnerability of citizens in a data-saturated ecosystem. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act of 2023, though a legislative response, remains consent-based in a context where digital literacy is asymmetric and informational asymmetry between data fiduciaries and data principals is structural. When the state or a private corporation collects biometric data, behavioral patterns, and location trails, the individual's autonomy — the very essence of dignity under Article 21 — is compromised not through overt coercion but through algorithmic opacity. The absence of algorithmic accountability mechanisms means that decisions affecting creditworthiness, employment, insurance, and even criminal profiling are made by systems whose logic is neither transparent nor contestable, raising serious questions under Article 14's guarantee of non-arbitrariness.

The internet shutdown regime further illustrates this duality. In Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020), the Supreme Court held that freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) includes the right to disseminate information through the internet, and that any restriction must satisfy the tests of proportionality and necessity. Yet India has consistently ranked among the highest in internet shutdowns globally, with Kashmir experiencing prolonged blackouts that severed access to education, healthcare, and livelihood. Technology here becomes the instrument through which the state exercises control, and the constitutional framework, while providing the vocabulary of rights, often fails to provide timely remedy. The Digital India Act, intended to replace the outdated IT Act of 2000, must address this tension by embedding proportionality as a structural principle rather than a judicial afterthought.

The digital divide compounds this duality along lines of class, gender, and geography. While urban India debates the ethics of generative AI and the India AI Mission allocates ₹10,372 crore for building computing infrastructure, nearly 40% of India's population lacks meaningful internet access. The BharatNet project, intended to connect 2.5 lakh gram panchayats with optical fiber, has faced persistent implementation deficits. This is not merely an infrastructure gap but a constitutional failure — a violation of the egalitarian promise of Article 14, where access to the digital public sphere determines access to rights, opportunities, and voice. The gendered dimension is particularly acute: the National Family Health Survey data reveals that only 33% of women in India have ever used the internet, compared to 57% of men. Technology, in this reading, does not merely reflect existing inequalities but actively reproduces and deepens them.

A critical evaluation must also acknowledge the counter-argument that technological determinism — the assumption that technology inherently produces certain outcomes — is analytically flawed. Technology is not autonomous; it is embedded in political economy. The same surveillance infrastructure that enables authoritarian control also enables pandemic contact tracing that saves lives. The same AI systems that perpetuate bias in hiring can also detect disease patterns invisible to human clinicians. The question is never technology per se but the governance architecture within which it operates. India's challenge is not that technology is dual-natured but that its regulatory framework remains reactive, fragmented, and insufficiently rights-based. The DPDP Act's exemption of government agencies from significant provisions, the absence of a dedicated AI regulator, and the lack of data localization enforcement mechanisms all point to a governance deficit rather than a technological one.

The way forward must therefore be specific and constitutionally grounded. First, the Digital India Act must incorporate a statutory right to internet access, drawing from the Anuradha Bhasin framework, with mandatory proportionality assessments before any shutdown. Second, algorithmic accountability legislation must be enacted, requiring impact assessments for all automated decision-making systems that affect fundamental rights, with provisions for human review and appeal. Third, the DPDP Act's consent framework must be supplemented with data fiduciary obligations that account for informational asymmetry, particularly for vulnerable populations. Fourth, BharatNet's implementation must be restructured with clear timelines, independent monitoring, and gender-disaggregated targets to address the digital divide as a rights issue. Fifth, the India AI Mission must allocate dedicated funding for bias auditing, open-source AI development, and public interest AI applications in health, agriculture, and education.

In conclusion, technology's duality is not an inherent property of tools but a reflection of the constitutional and governance choices that shape their deployment. The Indian Constitution, through its expansive interpretation of Articles 14, 19, and 21, provides a robust framework for ensuring that technology serves as an instrument of liberation rather than control. The Puttaswamy judgment's recognition of informational privacy, the Anuradha Bhasin ruling's protection of digital speech, and the directive principles' vision of equitable welfare together constitute a constitutional mandate for rights-based technological governance. The challenge for India is to move beyond reactive regulation toward a proactive constitutional technology policy — one that treats access as a right, accountability as non-negotiable, and innovation as inseparable from justice. The sword cuts both ways; the question is whether the Constitution is sharp enough to direct its edge.
